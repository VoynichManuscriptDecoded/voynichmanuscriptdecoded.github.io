The lira da braccio, the instrument played in Florence in the 1400s, had five strings over the fingerboard. One stroke indicates the first string, two strokes the second, and so on up to five. The existing i, ii, iii, iiii markers before d and n are therefore not harmonic ratios but string instructions, telling the practitioner precisely which string to sound at that solar moment. This reframes the entire timing and string system as a direct physical instruction set, consistent with the manuscript's core principle that the code is always simple and visual. 1st December 2025

Spent some time on the planetary/gallows glyphs, fully understand their shapes now. The glyph shapes are visual mnemonics - simple drawings that help you remember which planet is which by encoding what makes each one distinctive. The '4' pointing left (east) appears on Saturn, Jupiter, and Venus - these are reliable morning stars you can spot before sunrise. Mars and Mercury don't have the '4' because they're not stable eastern risers. Two lines touching the ground means "crosses the whole sky" - Mars and Saturn rise in the east and set in the west in one night. One line touching ground means "doesn't complete the crossing" - Mercury stays in twilight near the Sun, and Jupiter spends months looping backward high overhead instead of simply setting. Venus hugs the horizon (the '4' sits at ground level) because it never rises high. The loops show retrograde motion - small loops for Mars and Saturn's modest backward motion, a big curl for Jupiter's dramatic four-month loop. Each shape draws in one continuous stroke - trace it with your finger like a conductor's gesture, and the motion helps you remember which planet it represents.
